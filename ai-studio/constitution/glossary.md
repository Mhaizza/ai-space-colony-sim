# Glossary

Canonical definitions for every domain term used across this project. When a term appears in a design document, ADR, or code comment, it means exactly what is defined here. If a definition needs to change, update this file and open an ADR if the change affects existing systems.

---

## Agent

An autonomous entity in the simulation that perceives world state, evaluates its own internal state, and produces decisions without direct external instruction. All colonists are agents. The AI Director is also an agent. The player is *not* an agent — the player sends inputs; agents respond to world state.

---

## Colonist

A human agent living and working aboard the colony station. A colonist has needs, traits, relationships, a memory, and a goal stack. Colonists are the primary subjects of the simulation and the primary source of emergent drama.

---

## Module

A physical unit of the station — a room, structure, or installation. Modules have resource requirements (power, oxygen), capacity (beds, workstations), and states (functional, damaged, offline). Modules are the environment that colonists inhabit and the infrastructure the player builds and manages.

---

## Need

A quantified dimension of a colonist's wellbeing that decays over time and must be satisfied through appropriate actions. Examples: hunger, rest, safety, social contact, purpose. Needs drive agent decision-making: unmet needs raise urgency and eventually override lower-priority goals.

---

## Motivation

The underlying reason an agent is pursuing a goal. Where a *goal* is what an agent is trying to achieve, a *motivation* is why. Motivations are derived from trait weights and current need states. Two colonists may share a goal (eat) with different motivations (survival urgency vs. social habit).

---

## Trait

A persistent attribute that shapes how a colonist evaluates needs, forms relationships, and makes decisions. Traits are not behaviors — they are parameters that modify behavior systems. Examples: `resilient` (slower stress accumulation), `social` (higher weight on social needs), `perfectionist` (higher work quality, higher stress from failures).

---

## Goal

A specific, completable objective that an agent has committed to pursuing. Goals are generated by the agent's decision system based on current needs, motivations, and world state. Goals have priority, preconditions, and completion criteria. An agent may have multiple goals in a stack; the highest-priority actionable goal drives current behavior.

---

## Task

A discrete unit of work that satisfies (part of) a goal, tied to a specific location or module. Tasks are the atomic actions colonists perform: `RepairOxygenGenerator`, `EatInCafeteria`, `SleepInBunk`. Tasks have duration, resource cost, and skill requirements.

---

## Memory

A colonist's persistent record of significant events, interactions, and observations. Memory influences future decision-making — a colonist who witnessed a crisis remembers it and may adjust goal priorities accordingly. Memory is bounded (older, lower-significance entries decay). Memory is serialized as part of the colonist's save state.

---

## Relationship

A directional, weighted record of how one colonist regards another, derived from interaction history. Relationships have a sentiment score (positive to negative) and a familiarity score (strong to weak). Relationships influence cooperation, task assignment acceptance, stress propagation, and social need satisfaction.

---

## World State

The complete, authoritative snapshot of all simulation state at a given tick: all colonist states, all module states, all resource levels, all active events, and all relationship values. World state is the input to every agent decision and the output of every simulation tick. It is serializable and diffable.

---

## Simulation Tick

The fundamental unit of simulation time. On each tick, the simulation advances: resources are consumed and produced, needs decay, agents evaluate their state and make decisions, events are processed, and the new world state is produced. Tick rate is configurable and decoupled from render frame rate.

---

## Event

A discrete, timestamped occurrence in the simulation that other systems may react to. Events are fired by systems (e.g., `OxygenLow`, `ColonistDied`, `ModuleDamaged`) and consumed by agents, the AI Director, and the UI. Events are logged and contribute to colonist memory and player-visible history.

---

## Crisis

A sustained state of dangerous instability in one or more systems, typically caused by cascading failures or unmet resource demands. A crisis is not a single event — it is a condition that persists until resolved. The AI Director monitors for and responds to crises by adjusting event generation and escalating colonist stress.

---

## Resource

A consumable or producible quantity tracked at the colony level or module level. Examples: oxygen, power, water, food, spare parts. Resources flow through the simulation continuously; imbalances trigger events and affect colonist needs. Resources are not abstractions — they have production sources, consumption sinks, and storage limits.

---

## Colony

The full sociotechnical system: the station's physical infrastructure, its population of colonists, and the culture and relationships that emerge between them. The colony is what the player is managing. It is larger than the sum of its modules.

---

## Station

The physical structure of the colony — the collection of modules, corridors, and infrastructure that colonists inhabit. The station is the spatial layer of the simulation: it defines where things are, how modules connect, and what physical constraints govern movement and resource flow.

---

## Life Support

The set of systems that maintain the conditions necessary for colonist survival: oxygen generation and distribution, power, water recycling, temperature regulation, and food production. Life support systems are the highest-priority resource systems; failure in any life support system triggers immediate crises.

---

## Player

The human operating the game. The player does not control colonists directly. The player builds and modifies the station, sets policy (work schedules, resource priorities, module assignments), and responds to crises. The player's primary tool is environmental design; their primary feedback is the emergent behavior of the colony.

---

## AI Director

An invisible simulation agent that monitors colony-wide conditions and makes adjustments to event generation and crisis escalation to maintain dramatic tension and pacing. The AI Director does not control colonists — it nudges probabilities and selects from possible events. Its decisions are inspectable for the same reason colonist decisions are: explainability is non-negotiable.
