# Player Journey

How a single player experiences the game from first boot to late colony. This document describes the emotional and cognitive arc — not a tutorial script.

---

## First 10 Minutes — The Arrival

**Where the player is:**
The station is newly established. A small founding cohort of colonists has just arrived — 4 to 6 people. Life support is minimal but functional. The player's interface is sparse.

**What the player does:**
- Orients to the station layout: where are the modules, what systems are active
- Meets the colonists: scans their displayed attributes, watches their first behaviors
- Makes the first infrastructure decision: what to build next, or where to assign whom
- Discovers through observation that colonists have preferences and quirks — one is already in the break room, one is working longer than their shift, one seems to be following another around

**The emotional beat:**
Curiosity. The player is reading a new situation. There is no tutorial box telling them what matters. They notice things and form hypotheses. The station feels fragile but alive.

**Guided Discovery moment:**
The first time a colonist's need drops into the red and their work output visibly changes, the player understands the loop without being told: conditions affect behavior. They caused it by not building something fast enough. They now know what to do — not because the game told them, but because they observed.

**What the player should NOT feel:**
Overwhelmed. The founding cohort is small. The systems in play are few. The player has room to breathe and make mistakes without cascading consequences.

---

## First Hour — The Rhythm

**Where the player is:**
The colony has grown to 8–12 colonists. The player has made several infrastructure decisions. A few policies are in place. The first shift rotation has happened.

**What the player does:**
- Learns the scan habit: glancing at colonist states, watching resource trends, noticing anomalies
- Makes their first personnel decision — someone is in the wrong role, or two colonists are generating friction when assigned to the same module
- Deals with the first minor shortage: a resource that is trending down faster than expected
- Starts to identify 2–3 colonists as individuals — not stat blocks, but people with legible patterns

**The emotional beat:**
Investment. The player is beginning to feel responsible for these specific people. "The engineer who keeps working through rest periods" is now a character, not a unit. The player starts making decisions that account for individuals, not just aggregate need levels.

**The turning point:**
The first time a colonist does something unexpected — covered for someone, refused a task, formed a visible bond with another colonist — and the player's reaction is "I want to understand why that happened." They open the inspector not because they have to, but because they want to.

---

## First Colony Crisis — The Test

**What triggers it:**
Not a scripted event. A crisis emerges from the intersection of several building pressures: a resource margin that has been running thin, a colonist whose stress has been accumulating, an infrastructure gap the player deferred, a relationship tension they noticed but did not address.

**Typical first crisis shape:**
A critical system degrades (oxygen, power, or water) at the same time that the most qualified person to repair it is unavailable — because of their stress level, their current assignment, or a social conflict that hasn't been resolved.

**What the player does:**
- Triages: which problem to address first when multiple things are degrading simultaneously
- Improvises: makes decisions with incomplete information under pressure
- Accepts costs: saving the station may require sacrificing something — a relationship, a colonist's wellbeing, a planned infrastructure project

**The emotional beat:**
The player feels the weight of responsibility for the first time. Not panic — the game doesn't demand speed — but the recognition that the decisions they made in the last hour are directly causing the situation they are in now. This is the moment the game becomes real.

**The post-mortem:**
After the crisis resolves, the player can trace the full cause chain. Every link is visible. The player knows exactly what they would do differently. They start the next cycle with that knowledge.

---

## Mid-Game — The Colony Has a Character

**Where the player is:**
16–20 colonists. Multiple shift rotations. Several crises navigated. The colony has a history.

**What is different from early game:**
- The colony's social structure is visible: informal leaders have emerged, cliques have formed, certain colonists have elevated or reduced standing based on their behavior under pressure
- The player's early decisions have become constraints: a module that was built in a suboptimal location is now load-bearing and cannot easily be changed; a policy that worked when there were 8 colonists is now causing friction at 16
- Problems compound: a relationship that was strained in hour two is now a structural liability
- New colonists arrive into an existing culture they must navigate

**What the player does:**
- Manages the colony as a system of people, not just a set of resource flows
- Adapts policies that no longer fit the colony's current composition
- Navigates interpersonal situations that no single decision can resolve — they require sustained attention over several in-game days
- Watches the colony tell stories that no one designed

**The emotional beat:**
Ownership. The player has a specific colony that is unlike any playthrough guide. They understand its history. They have opinions about its colonists. They are responsible for decisions made two hours ago that are still echoing.

---

## Late Game — Consequences as Architecture

**Where the player is:**
Near the colonist cap (24). All life-support systems are established. The colony has been through multiple crises. Its culture is calcified.

**What is different from mid-game:**
- The resource layer is largely solved — it requires maintenance, not reinvention
- The people layer has become the full game: social structures, long-term relationships, colonists who have developed significantly from their arrival state
- Early decisions are structural: the colony's power dynamics, work culture, and informal hierarchies were shaped by what the player did in the first hour
- New crises are social in origin, not resource-driven: a faction that has accumulated resentment, a colonist whose standing has given them too much influence, a cultural norm that is now producing systematic problems

**What the player does:**
- Works with (or against) the colony's emergent culture
- Makes decisions that will outlast any individual crisis: adjusting the social architecture of the station
- Considers what the colony will look like in another 20 in-game hours — and whether it can sustain itself

**The emotional beat:**
The player feels the full weight of their history with this colony. They know these people. They understand why the colony is the way it is. Success is not optimization — it is a colony that is still alive, still legible, still generating stories they want to watch.

**The long-term question the player asks:**
"If I could go back to the first hour, what would I do differently?" — and they have a specific answer. That answer is the game.
