# Gameplay Phases

The phases of a single colony playthrough. These are not locked stages — the colony moves between them based on conditions, not timers. A well-managed colony may skip or compress phases. A struggling colony may collapse without ever reaching stabilization. A colony can regress: stabilization → crisis → early-game-scale triage.

Phases are named from the player's perspective, not the colony's.

---

## Phase: Early Game

**Colony state:** 4–8 colonists. All founding members. Life support is manually configured and barely adequate. Everything is fragile.

**Player's primary task:** Establish a survivable foundation without knowing which decisions will matter.

**What the player controls:**
- Which modules to build first and in what order
- How to assign the founding colonists to roles
- What shift schedule to establish
- Which resource shortfalls to accept and which to address first

**What the player cannot yet see:**
- How colonist personalities will interact under pressure
- Which early decisions will have long-term social consequences
- Where the first crisis will originate

**The dominant feeling:**
Careful optimism. The colony is fragile, but the player has room to learn. Mistakes have consequences that are small enough to recover from — or small enough to teach without punishing.

**Phase ends when:**
The life-support foundation is stable enough that the player's attention shifts from "will we survive the next cycle" to "who are these people."

**Risk of skipping this phase too fast:**
Players who rush past the early relationship signals miss the context for every social crisis that follows. The game must make early colonist behavior legible enough to reward attention — not so urgent that it forces a tutorial read.

---

## Phase: Expansion

**Colony state:** 8–16 colonists. New arrivals joining an existing social structure. Resource systems are scaling.

**Player's primary task:** Grow the colony without breaking what already works.

**What the player controls:**
- When to accept new colonists and how many at once
- Which new modules to build to support growth
- How to integrate new arrivals into existing shift structures
- Whether to adjust policies that worked at 8 colonists but may not work at 14

**What is changing:**
- New colonists arrive with their own traits, needs, and relationships — they are strangers to an established social group
- Resource systems that were adequate are now under increased load
- The player's attention is divided across more people than before
- Social dynamics that were simple at 8 colonists are becoming more complex

**The dominant feeling:**
Managed tension. The player is making more decisions than before. Some decisions are clearly right; many require genuine trade-offs. The colony is generating more stories — and some of them are starting to worry the player.

**Phase ends when:**
Growth has stopped or slowed, and the player's challenge shifts from "how do I integrate new people" to "how do I manage the society I have built."

**Risk:**
Expansion too fast creates social instability the player cannot manage. Expansion too slow leaves resources unused and development stalled. The game should make both failure modes legible — not punish one and reward the other automatically.

---

## Phase: Stabilization

**Colony state:** 14–20 colonists. No new arrivals or very slow intake. The colony has survived its first crises.

**Player's primary task:** Understand and work with the colony that has actually formed — not the one the player intended to build.

**What is true of this colony:**
- It has a social structure the player did not design: informal leaders, cliques, tension lines, cultural norms that formed from accumulated behavior
- Several of the player's early decisions are now structural constraints
- Some colonists have changed significantly from their arrival state — relationships have shaped them
- Resource systems are functional but require ongoing maintenance

**What the player does:**
- Audits the colony's current state: who holds informal authority, which relationships are liabilities, which systems are quietly degrading
- Makes adjustments to policies and infrastructure that fit the actual colony, not the intended one
- Chooses which problems to accept as permanent and which to actively address

**The dominant feeling:**
Reckoning. The player sees the gap between what they tried to build and what they actually built. This is not failure — it is the point. The colony is a legible record of every decision, and stabilization is the first moment the player has the distance to read it clearly.

**Phase ends when:**
The colony is sustainable without active intervention for extended periods — and the player's attention is now fully on the people rather than the systems.

---

## Phase: Optimization (optional, not guaranteed)

**Colony state:** A colony that has reached a stable operational state. Life support is reliable. Social dynamics are not in acute crisis.

**Player's primary task:** Understand the colony deeply enough to improve it deliberately.

**What the player does:**
- Identifies the colony's structural weaknesses and decides which to address
- Makes deliberate long-term investments: building relationships between isolated colonists, adjusting the culture by changing conditions, preparing for anticipated future crises
- Experiments with policies and observes outcomes over extended periods

**What the game must deliver here:**
- The resource layer must not be fully solvable. If it can be optimized to zero-risk, optimization becomes the final boss and the people layer loses relevance.
- New social dynamics must still be generating novel situations even after the player has "seen everything" in the resource layer.

**The dominant feeling:**
Mastery — but not completion. The player who is genuinely good at this game does not solve the colony; they understand it deeply enough to navigate it skillfully. Optimization is not a destination; it is a sustained state of informed attention.

**Phase ends when:**
A new crisis disrupts the stable state — which it always will.

---

## Phase: Crisis

**Colony state:** A significant system is failing or has failed. Multiple cascading problems may be active simultaneously.

**Player's primary task:** Triage. Decide what to protect, what to sacrifice, and what to accept losing.

**Where crises come from:**
- Resource system failure (equipment degradation, supply shortage, overcapacity)
- Social cascade (an interpersonal conflict that has grown to involve multiple colonists or factions)
- External pressure (environmental, infrastructural, or arrival of new colonists at a bad time)
- Compound failure (multiple minor problems that became critical simultaneously)

**What the player does:**
- Reads the situation fast enough to understand the priority order
- Makes decisions under incomplete information — the crisis may reveal things about colonist relationships or system states that were not previously visible
- Accepts costs: every meaningful crisis involves a genuine trade-off, not a solution that loses nothing

**What the game must never do:**
- Produce a crisis the player could not have anticipated from available information
- Produce a crisis with no survivable path (unless the player has reached an unrecoverable state through accumulated decisions — which is different from an instant death)
- Make the crisis resolution feel like a puzzle with a right answer

**The dominant feeling:**
The weight of consequence. The player is living inside the results of their previous decisions. The crisis is not random — it is the culmination of a chain the player can trace.

**Phase ends when:**
The acute crisis resolves — one way or another. The colony may emerge weakened, changed, or intact. It does not emerge unchanged.

---

## Phase: Endgame

**Colony state:** Near or at the colonist cap (24). The colony has a fully formed culture and history. Major crises have been navigated.

**Note:** Endgame is not a timer or a win condition. It is a colony state.

**Player's primary task:** Understand and accept what this colony has become.

**What is true at endgame:**
- The resource layer is a maintenance task, not a design challenge
- The people layer is the entire game
- The colony's culture has calcified: changing it requires working against accumulated social inertia
- Some colonists have long histories with each other — their relationships are load-bearing in ways that were never planned
- The player has strong opinions about specific colonists — who is valuable, who is a liability, who they are rooting for

**What the player does:**
- Manages the social architecture of a living community
- Decides whether to lean into the colony's culture or resist it
- Prepares for crises that are now social in origin rather than resource-driven
- Eventually: decides what this colony's legacy means to them

**The question the game asks at endgame:**
Not "did you win?" — but "what kind of colony did you build, and what does that tell you about the decisions you made?"

**A successful endgame:**
A colony that is still alive, still generating stories the player wants to watch, and whose history the player could recount in detail. The player can answer: what went wrong, what went unexpectedly right, and what they would do differently.

**A failed endgame:**
Colony collapse — the cascade that the player could not stop. This is not a punishment. It is the conclusion of a traceable narrative. The post-mortem is the last act of the game.
