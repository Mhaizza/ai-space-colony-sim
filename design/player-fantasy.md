# Player Fantasy

---

## Who Is the Player?

The player is the **Colony Administrator** — an offscreen authority responsible for the station's survival and the welfare of everyone aboard. They are not a colonist. They are not a god. They are the person who makes the institutional decisions: what gets built, how resources flow, who works where, what the policy is when things go wrong.

Think: the director of a remote research station, the administrator of a space habitat, the person who gets the call at 3am when the generator fails — and who made the staffing decisions that determined whether anyone qualified was awake to fix it.

The player is **responsible without being omnipotent**. They have broad powers and very limited direct control.

---

## What Fantasy Are They Fulfilling?

### The Architect Fantasy
> "I designed the conditions. Everything that happened was a consequence of choices I made."

The player builds a world and watches people live in it. The satisfaction comes not from executing a plan perfectly, but from watching the plan interact with reality — and discovering which of your assumptions were wrong.

This is not "I won." It is "I understand what happened here."

### The Caretaker Fantasy
> "These are my people. I'm responsible for them."

The player develops genuine investment in specific colonists — not because the game asks them to, but because those colonists become legible through behavior over time. When a colonist the player has "known" for 40 hours makes a catastrophic mistake under pressure, the player feels it. Not because a sad music cue played, but because they understand exactly why it happened and what role they played in creating those conditions.

### The Systems Thinker Fantasy
> "I can see how this works. And I can see where it's about to break."

The player who loves this game loves reading the simulation. They track need levels, watch relationship graphs, notice the colonist who is three decisions away from a breakdown. The fantasy is competence — not the ability to prevent all bad outcomes, but the ability to understand them deeply enough to navigate them.

---

## What Decisions Should the Player Make?

These are the core decisions the game is designed around. Every major system should feed into at least one of these.

**Infrastructure decisions**
- What to build, where to build it, in what order.
- How to route resources between modules.
- What capacity to maintain for each life-support system.

**Policy decisions**
- Work shift lengths and rotation schedules.
- Resource rationing levels during shortages.
- Prioritization rules: who gets scarce resources first.
- Response protocols for recurring crisis types.

**Personnel decisions**
- Who is assigned to which module or role.
- Whether to promote, reassign, or leave someone in a suboptimal role.
- How to respond when two colonists cannot work together.
- When to override a colonist's preferred assignment versus respecting it.

**Crisis decisions**
- Which problem to address first when multiple crises compete for limited resources.
- Whether to sacrifice a minor system to protect a critical one.
- When to accept a bad outcome now to prevent a worse one later.

**Inaction decisions**
- Choosing not to intervene and letting the colonists resolve something themselves.
- Recognizing when a situation will resolve on its own versus when it requires attention.
- Accepting that some colonist preferences will go unsatisfied by design.

---

## What Decisions Should the Player NEVER Make?

These decisions would undermine the game's core design. If a feature asks the player to make these decisions, the feature is wrong.

**"Tell this specific colonist to do this specific thing right now."**
Direct individual orders eliminate the AI layer. If the player can command colonists, colonists become tools. The game becomes an RTS with extra steps.

**"Fix this colonist's mood by spending a resource."**
Mood is a consequence, not a resource. Allowing players to purchase morale treats the colonist's inner state as a vending machine. The only legitimate path to improved morale is improving the conditions that caused it to drop.

**"Set this colonist's personality trait."**
Colonist traits are not character creation options. They are discovered through observation. The player should not know everything about a colonist at intake — they should learn who a colonist is by watching them under pressure.

**"Rewind time to erase a decision."**
The simulation never rewinds. The player may restore an earlier saved timeline, but that timeline becomes a different history — it does not erase the previous one. A colony that survived because the player reloaded a save is a different colony from the one that collapsed. Both histories are real; neither is undone. Save/load is permitted; consequence is not optional.

**"Optimize away all risk."**
The simulation is designed to always present the player with situations where all options have costs. A player who finds a zero-downside solution to a recurring problem has either found an exploit or the design has failed. The player should always be managing tradeoffs, never eliminating them.

---

## What Emotions Should the Game Create?

### Primary — Responsibility
Not guilt. Not dread. *Responsibility.* The feeling of being the person who is accountable for what happens here. This emotion motivates engagement — it makes the player care about outcomes before they happen, not just react to them after.

### Primary — Investment in Individuals
The player should develop genuine attachment to specific colonists through observed behavior over time — not through tutorials that say "this is your favorite colonist." Attachment that is earned feels real. Attachment that is assigned feels hollow. When a colonist the player has watched for hours fails, it should hurt.

### Secondary — Mastery
The satisfaction of understanding a complex system well enough to navigate it skillfully. Not dominance — mastery. A master player still faces crises. They just understand them faster, see the chains forming earlier, make better decisions about which costs to accept.

### Secondary — Wonder
The colonists should occasionally surprise the player. A behavior combination that produces an unexpected but completely logical outcome. A relationship that developed in a direction nobody designed. The feeling of "I didn't write that story — but I watched it happen in a simulation I helped create."

### Intentional Absence — Frustration from Opacity
The game should never produce the emotion of "I don't know what's happening and I can't find out." Confusion is acceptable; confusion paired with inaccessible information is not. Every negative emotion the game produces should be traceable to a player decision, not to a hidden system.

### Intentional Absence — Boredom from Solved States
Once a player fully solves the resource layer, the people layer must still generate new problems. The game should never reach a state where the player has nothing to read, nothing to manage, no relationship on the edge of something. Stasis is a design failure.

---

## The Test

A design decision passes the player fantasy test if it can complete this sentence:

> "This feature makes the player feel more like the **architect of conditions** responsible for the survival and welfare of autonomous people they've come to know through observation."

If the feature makes the player feel like a puppeteer, an optimizer, or a passive observer of authored events — it fails the test.
