# Game Vision

---

## Mission

Build the colony simulation where people are the system.

Not resource spreadsheets with faces on them. Not a city-builder where citizens are traffic. A game where every colonist has a memory, a fear, a reason to get up in the morning — and where the player's job is to create the conditions for those people to thrive, not to puppeteer them into compliance.

---

## Elevator Pitch

> You're not in charge. You're responsible.

**AI Space Colony Simulator** is a sci-fi colony management game set aboard a deep-space station. The player builds infrastructure, sets policy, and manages crises. The colonists — fully autonomous AI agents — do everything else. They form friendships and rivalries, pursue their own goals, break under pressure, and occasionally surprise you.

The colony doesn't fail because you made a bad build. It fails because you didn't pay attention to the people.

---

## Genre

**Primary:** Colony simulation / management
**Secondary:** Emergent narrative / systemic storytelling
**Reference points (what we learn from, not what we copy):**
- *Dwarf Fortress* — depth of simulation, the stories it generates
- *RimWorld* — readability of colonist drama, crisis pacing
- *Oxygen Not Included* — life support as the central tension
- *Crusader Kings III* — characters as the engine of drama
- *Papers, Please* — the weight of institutional decisions on individual lives

We are not a city-builder. We are not a survival game. We are not a narrative game with branching scripts. We are a **people simulator** set in space.

---

## Design Goals

**1. Every colonist should feel like a person.**
After one hour of play, the player should be able to describe at least two colonists by personality — not by job title or stat block. "Maya is the one who keeps covering for Ren even though it tanks her own productivity" is the target. "Engineer #4 has low morale" is not.

**2. The player should feel like an architect, not a puppeteer.**
The player's power is environmental and institutional. They build rooms, assign shifts, set ration levels, and respond to crises. They do not click on a colonist and say "be happy." The colonist's happiness is a consequence, not a button.

**3. Failure should be a story, not a game over screen.**
A collapsing colony is not a punishment. It is a narrative. The player should be able to trace exactly why it happened — not because the game told them, but because the logic was always visible.

**4. Complexity should emerge, not be designed in.**
Every piece of depth in this game should come from two or more simple systems interacting. If a designer had to write a special case for it, it's probably wrong.

**5. The game should be honest about its AI.**
The player can always ask "why did this colonist do that?" and receive a true answer. The simulation has nothing to hide.

## Long-Term Vision

### Phase 1 — The First Colony
A single station. Hard cap of 24 colonists (Phase 1 target). Architecture must remain scalable beyond 24 without redesign — the cap is a gameplay decision, not a structural one. Core life-support loop: oxygen, power, water, food, rest. Colonist needs and basic AI decision-making visible to the player via Guided Discovery — not tutorial prompts, but observable consequences that teach through play. The game is survivable and the drama is readable.

### Phase 2 — The Human Element
Relationships, factions, long-term memory. Colonists begin to form their own social structures independent of the player's intentions. Crises escalate not just from resource failure but from interpersonal dynamics. The player experiences this through observable phenomena — colony conditions, environmental pressure, political climate, station wear, external signals — not through any visible system named or explained as a director or controller.

### Phase 3 — The Colony Has a Culture
Multiple stations, intercolony politics. The player's early decisions have calcified into colony traditions and social norms that new colonists must navigate. The game has a post-launch modding layer.

## Target Audience

### Primary
Strategy and management players who feel that most colony sims are "too mechanical" — who want the depth of Dwarf Fortress but not the opacity, who want the character of RimWorld but not the feeling that stories are authored rather than discovered.

Age range: broadly 20–40. Comfortable with complexity. Values systems over action. Reads patch notes.

### Secondary
Players drawn to AI and emergent systems as a subject. People who will open the colonist inspector not because they have to, but because they want to understand why. The simulation is legible enough to be interesting on a mechanical level.

### Not Our Audience
- Players who want fast feedback loops and moment-to-moment action.
- Players who want a power fantasy where they can solve everything.
- Players who want a tightly authored story with defined characters and endings.

## Success Metrics

### The Dinner Table Test
A player recounts a story from their colony to someone who has never played the game — and that person is genuinely interested. The story has characters, cause and effect, and an emotional beat.

### The Post-Mortem Test
After a colony fails, the player understands why. Not because a pop-up told them. Because they watched it happen and can explain the causal chain.

### The Screenshot Test
Players voluntarily share colony screenshots, annotated with colonist names and drama, without being prompted by a game mechanic to do so.

### The Return Test
Players start a new colony after a failure. The failure taught them something specific about how to design conditions differently — not just "build more oxygen generators."

## What This Game Is NOT

**Not a survival game.**
Survival games put the player in danger. This game puts the colony in danger. The player is never at risk — their colonists are. That distance is intentional and essential.

**Not a city-builder.**
City-builders optimize flow. This game is about people, not traffic. A colonist who refuses to walk the efficient route because they're avoiding someone is a feature, not a pathfinding bug.

**Not an RTS.**
There is no real-time unit control. The player issues no direct orders to colonists. Speed and reflex are not the skill being tested.

**Not a narrative game.**
There are no authored story events with named characters and scripted outcomes. Every story is generated. The game ships with no protagonist.

**Not a god game.**
The player cannot reach into the simulation and move colonists like chess pieces. They can create conditions. They cannot create outcomes.

**Not a "fun hard" game.**
Difficulty is not the point. Understanding is the point. A colony that survives easily because the player understood the systems is a success. A colony that collapses despite the player's best efforts, in a way the player cannot understand, is a design failure.
